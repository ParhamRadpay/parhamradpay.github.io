Research

Functional renormalization and quantum spin glasses

Research in this direction develops functional renormalization group methods for disordered quantum many-body systems, with particular emphasis on mean-field quantum spin-glass models in the large-N limit. Special attention was given to coarse-graining procedures adapted to disorder, including renormalization along spectral variables associated with random quadratic operators, which provide access to long-time dynamics and low-energy behavior.

Within this framework, finite-scale singularities in renormalization-group flows are studied as possible indicators of glassy physics, metastability, and symmetry breaking. Non-perturbative approaches are employed to investigate low-temperature phase structure and to obtain controlled descriptions of dynamical and thermodynamic transitions in quantum disordered systems.

Random tensors, geometry, and quantum entanglement

A complementary research direction investigates the geometric structure of random antisymmetric tensors and their interpretation as fermionic quantum states. The injective norm of these tensors characterizes multipartite geometric entanglement and establishes connections between high-dimensional probability, random matrix theory, and quantum information.

Analytical approaches based on Kac–Rice methods on Grassmann manifolds provide asymptotic formulas and high-probability bounds for the injective norm in both fixed-particle and proportional-scaling regimes. This framework reveals particle–hole dualities related to Hodge-star symmetry and demonstrates that random fermionic states are typically highly entangled in large dimensions.

Broader perspective

More broadly, my research explores how disorder, randomness, and high dimensionality generate new forms of complexity across statistical physics, quantum many-body theory, and information geometry. By combining renormalization, probabilistic techniques, and geometric analysis, I aim to build conceptual bridges between glassy dynamics, tensorial complexity, and quantum entanglement.
